OIL MARKETS: BRENT CRUDE PLUNGES ON HORMUZ HOPES

Oil prices posted their sharpest single-day decline in months after conflicting signals emerged from Iran and Washington about a potential de-escalation in the Strait of Hormuz standoff. Brent crude fell 9.1% to approximately $90/bbl, while WTI settled near $84/bbl. European natural gas tumbled 10% to €39/MWh.

Iran announced the Strait of Hormuz would reopen, but immediately qualified the statement — the opening would exclude ships from "hostile" countries. Tehran also threatened to close the strait again if the US blockade continues. President Trump separately confirmed that US peace talks are being planned, but offered no confirmation from the Iranian side.

Most major shipowners are adopting a wait-and-see approach. The International Association of Independent Tanker Owners recommended members avoid the region entirely until the situation clarifies. Industry sources describe the situation as "the first penguin problem" — no one wants to be the first to test whether the routes are truly safe.

---

RATES & TREASURIES: YIELDS HIT ONE-MONTH LOWS

Falling energy prices reignited expectations for Federal Reserve rate cuts, sending Treasury yields to their lowest levels in a month. Markets are repricing the probability of a June cut upward as the energy inflation impulse fades. Bond traders noted that if Iranian de-escalation holds, the disinflationary tailwind could pull forward the Fed's timeline meaningfully.

---

GEOPOLITICS: BEIJING BECOMES THE NEW DIPLOMATIC HUB

Xi Jinping hosted an unprecedented five high-level diplomatic meetings this week as world leaders scramble to hedge against US unpredictability. Leaders from Europe, Southeast Asia, and Latin America visited Beijing seeking bilateral trade frameworks that don't depend on Washington's goodwill.

Spain and Brazil are reportedly building an informal anti-Trump economic alliance, exploring coordinated tariff responses and supply chain diversification away from US-dependent networks.

---

TECHNOLOGY: QUANTUM COMPUTING GETS ITS MOMENT

Nvidia's high-profile backing of Xanadu, a Canadian quantum computing firm, sent the sector into a brief frenzy. Xanadu's founder became a billionaire on paper as the company's valuation surged following the announcement. Analysts remain divided on whether the Nvidia endorsement reflects genuine near-term commercial potential or is primarily a positioning move ahead of the next compute arms race.

---

MACRO: IMF FLAGS CANADA AS G-7'S STRONGEST FISCAL POSITION

The International Monetary Fund's latest Fiscal Monitor ranked Canada as having the strongest fiscal position among G-7 nations, citing its relatively low debt-to-GDP ratio and stable revenue base. Analysts noted this could make Canadian assets a defensive play amid broader macro uncertainty.
